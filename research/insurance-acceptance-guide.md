# Insurance Acceptance Guide — Community Acupuncture in Portland, OR

**Prepared:** 2026-04-07
**For:** Yarrow (Yarree) — POCA-Model Community Acupuncture
**Context:** Process, requirements, and strategic considerations for accepting health insurance

---

## Overview: Why This Matters

Oregon law (ORS 743A.078) **mandates acupuncture coverage** for all commercial health plans. This means most insured Portlanders already have acupuncture benefits. The question isn't whether patients have coverage — it's whether accepting insurance makes sense for a community acupuncture practice.

This document covers the full process, then provides a strategic recommendation tailored to Yarrow's POCA model.

## The Credentialing Process (Becoming an In-Network Provider)

### Prerequisites — What You Need First

| Requirement | Details | Cost | Timeline |
|-------------|---------|------|----------|
| Oregon LAc License | Active license from Oregon Medical Board | ~$325 | Already in plan |
| NCCAOM Certification | Diplomate status (required for OR licensure anyway) | ~$100/year maintenance | Already in plan |
| NPI Number (Type 1) | Individual provider identifier from CMS (nppes.cms.hhs.gov) | Free | Immediate online |
| NPI Number (Type 2) | Organizational NPI for the practice entity | Free | After business entity formed |
| EIN | From IRS, for the business entity | Free | Already in plan |
| Malpractice Insurance | $1M/$3M occurrence-based policy minimum | $300–$800/year | Already in plan (increase to $1M/$3M if needed) |
| Business Entity | LLC or PLLC formed with Oregon Secretary of State | ~$100 | Already in plan |
| Taxonomy Code | **171100000X** (Acupuncturist) — goes on NPI application | N/A | Part of NPI setup |

Most of these are already on the startup checklist. The **additional steps** for insurance are the NPI numbers, CAQH profile, and insurer applications.

### Step-by-Step Credentialing

**Step 1: CAQH ProView Profile**
- Create an account at caqh.org
- This is the universal credentialing database — most commercial insurers pull your data from here
- Upload: license, malpractice certificate, education transcripts, NCCAOM cert, practice info
- Must re-attest every 120 days to stay active
- Cost: Free

**Step 2: Apply to Each Insurer's Provider Network**
- Even with CAQH, you submit a separate application to each payer
- Some have online portals, some still use paper/fax
- Apply through each insurer's "provider relations" or "network management" department

**Step 3: Wait for Credentialing Review**
- This is the bottleneck: **60–180 days per insurer**
- Follow up every 2–3 weeks — applications stall without nudging

**Step 4: Contract Review**
- Insurer sends a provider agreement with a fee schedule
- Review carefully — you can sometimes negotiate rates
- Consider having a healthcare attorney review your first contract

**Step 5: Get Your Effective Date**
- You cannot bill until your effective date
- Claims for services before the effective date will be denied
- **Start credentialing 4–6 months before you want to see insured patients**

### Credentialing Timeline

| Step | Duration |
|------|----------|
| Gather documents, get NPI, get malpractice | 1–2 weeks |
| Complete CAQH ProView profile | 1–2 weeks |
| Submit applications to insurers | 1 week |
| Insurer credentialing review | **60–180 days** (bottleneck) |
| Contract review and signing | 1–4 weeks |
| **Total from start to first billable date** | **3–6 months** |

## Oregon-Specific Insurance Landscape

### ORS 743A.078 — The Acupuncture Mandate

Oregon requires **all commercial health insurers** to include acupuncture coverage. This means:
- Insurers cannot categorically exclude acupuncture
- They CAN impose visit limits (typically 12–30/year), copays, prior auth, and diagnosis restrictions
- Applies to plans regulated by Oregon DCBS (Division of Financial Regulation)

### What's NOT Covered by the Mandate
- **Self-insured employer plans (ERISA)** — federal law, exempt from state mandates. Many large Portland employers (Nike, Intel) have these. Many DO cover acupuncture voluntarily, but it's not required.
- **Medicare** — federal program, not subject to state mandates
- **Medicaid/OHP** — determined by the state Medicaid program and CCOs

## Billing & Coding Basics

### CPT Codes for Acupuncture

| Code | Description | Typical Reimbursement |
|------|-------------|-----------------------|
| **97810** | Acupuncture without e-stim, initial 15 min face-to-face | $40–$70 |
| **97811** | Each additional 15 min without e-stim (add-on) | $30–$50 |
| **97813** | Acupuncture WITH e-stim, initial 15 min face-to-face | $45–$75 |
| **97814** | Each additional 15 min with e-stim (add-on) | $35–$55 |

### Critical Rule: Face-to-Face Time

These are **time-based codes** requiring documented "personal one-on-one contact with the patient." This is the key tension with community acupuncture:

- Billable time = ONLY time actively at the patient's side (intake, needling, checking, removing needles)
- NOT the time the patient rests with needles retained
- In community acupuncture, you may only have 10–15 min of face-to-face per patient = **1 unit (97810) per visit**
- Private practice: 45–60 min session = 3–4 units per visit

### Other Billing Requirements

- **ICD-10 diagnosis codes** on every claim (M54.5 low back pain, M54.2 cervicalgia, G43.909 migraine, etc.)
- **SOAP notes** for every patient, every visit — chief complaint, history, exam, diagnosis, treatment, time, points used
- **CMS-1500 form** format for claims (submitted electronically via a clearinghouse)
- **Place of Service code 11** (Office) for community acupuncture

### Fee Schedule Setup

Set your "usual and customary" (UCR) charges **at or above** the highest rate you'd ever charge:
- Recommended: $100–$150 for initial visit, $75–$100 for follow-up
- This is your billed rate, NOT what you collect from cash patients
- If your UCR is lower than the insurer's allowed amount, you leave money on the table

### Billing Software Options

| Software | Cost | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| Jane App | $79–$399/mo | Popular for acupuncture in PNW. Scheduling + charting + billing. |
| Unified Practice | Varies | Built specifically for acupuncture/TCM. |
| Office Ally | Free | Budget option. Free claims submission. |
| SimplePractice | ~$69/mo | Also works for acupuncture. |

You'll also need a **clearinghouse** ($30–$100/mo or per-claim) to transmit electronic claims. Most billing software integrates with one.

## The Community Acupuncture Dilemma

### The Core Tension

The POCA model (high volume, low cost, sliding scale, group room) is fundamentally designed as a **cash-pay model**. Insurance billing creates friction with this model in specific ways:

**1. Low Per-Visit Reimbursement**
- Community model = ~1 billing unit per visit = $40–$60 from insurance
- Private practice model = 3–4 units per visit = $120–$280 from insurance
- Your sliding scale average ($35) is close to what insurance pays for 1 unit anyway

**2. Documentation Burden**
- Insurance requires individual SOAP notes for every patient, every visit
- At 4–8 patients/hour, this is a LOT of charting
- This is the #1 reason POCA clinics avoid insurance

**3. Sliding Scale Complications**
- Insurers expect your UCR charge to be at or above the highest amount you charge
- If you bill insurance at $75 but your sliding scale goes to $25, that's fine
- But charging insured patients MORE than cash patients can create ethical/contractual tension

**4. Administrative Overhead**
- Benefits verification, claims submission, denial follow-up, appeals, reauthorizations
- Estimated 30–40% of staff time in insurance-heavy practices
- May need a part-time biller ($18–$30/hr) or outsource (5–10% of insurance collections)

**5. Slow Payment**
- 30–90 days for claims payment (vs. cash at time of service)
- Possible clawbacks and retroactive denials

### What POCA Says

POCA has historically been skeptical of insurance billing for community acupuncture. Their position: the administrative cost undermines the high-volume, low-overhead model that makes community acupuncture affordable and sustainable. Many successful POCA clinics are cash-only with sliding scales.

That said, some POCA members do accept insurance — usually selectively.

## Strategic Options for Yarrow

### Option A: Cash-Only with Superbills (Recommended to Start)

**How it works:**
- Operate fully on sliding scale ($25–$50)
- Give patients a **superbill** — an itemized receipt with CPT codes, ICD-10 codes, your NPI, and practice info
- Patients submit superbills to their insurer for **out-of-network reimbursement**
- You collect your sliding scale fee at time of service

**Pros:**
- Zero administrative burden from insurance
- No credentialing wait time
- Patients can still use their benefits (partially)
- Preserves the POCA model exactly as designed
- No documentation requirements beyond your own clinical notes

**Cons:**
- Patients get lower out-of-network reimbursement (if any — some plans have no OON benefits)
- Patients must do the paperwork themselves
- You're invisible in provider directories

**Cost to implement:** Near zero. Just create a superbill template.

### Option B: Selective In-Network (2–3 Insurers)

**How it works:**
- Credential with Regence and Providence only (covers a large share of Portland)
- Bill insurance for those patients; everyone else pays sliding scale
- Maintain the same community room setup

**Pros:**
- Appears in provider directories for 2 major insurers
- Captures insured patients who wouldn't come otherwise
- Revenue boost: even $50/visit from insurance is higher than your $35 average

**Cons:**
- Documentation burden for insured patients
- Need billing software + clearinghouse ($100–$200/mo)
- Credentialing takes 3–6 months
- Creates a two-track system (insured patients = more charting, cash patients = less)

**Cost to implement:** ~$100–$200/month ongoing + ~$1,500 if using a credentialing service

### Option C: Hybrid Model (Private Room + Community Room)

**How it works:**
- Community room operates on sliding scale (cash/superbill)
- Add a small private treatment room for insurance patients
- Private room = longer face-to-face time = 2–3 billable units = $80–$150 per visit
- Bill insurance for private room; community room stays cash

**Pros:**
- Maximizes insurance revenue per visit (more billable units in private setting)
- Cleaner separation between insurance and cash operations
- Offers patients a choice
- The existing business model already mentions an "ideal setup" with a small private room

**Cons:**
- Requires slightly larger space (add ~100 sq ft for private room)
- Still need billing infrastructure
- Splits your attention between two models

**Cost to implement:** Slightly higher rent + billing costs

### Option D: OHP/Medicaid Focus (Mission-Aligned)

**How it works:**
- Credential specifically with Health Share of Oregon (Portland's main CCO)
- Serve OHP patients who can't even afford the $25 sliding scale minimum
- Low reimbursement ($25–$45) but high mission alignment

**Pros:**
- Serves the most underserved population
- Aligns with Yarrow's values (equity, access, queer/trans community)
- Many OHP patients in Yarrow's target neighborhoods (Cully, Lents, Rockwood)

**Cons:**
- Very low reimbursement — may not cover administrative costs
- CCO credentialing can be complex
- Additional documentation requirements

## Recommendation: Phased Approach

### Phase 1 — Launch (Fall 2026): Option A — Cash-Only with Superbills
- Focus on building patient volume and nailing the POCA model
- Create a superbill template (include CPT codes, ICD-10, NPI, practice info)
- Begin NPI application and CAQH profile during pre-launch (no cost, useful later)
- **Don't let insurance distract from the core business launch**

### Phase 2 — Growth (Spring 2027): Evaluate & Begin Credentialing
- After 6 months of operation, assess:
  - How many patients ask about insurance?
  - What insurers do your patients have?
  - Can you handle the current documentation load?
- If demand warrants: begin credentialing with Regence and Providence (Option B)
- Timeline: apply in Spring 2027, credentialed by Fall 2027

### Phase 3 — Established (Fall 2027+): Expand as Needed
- Add OHP/Health Share credentialing if serving underserved communities (Option D)
- Consider hybrid model if revenue goals require it (Option C)
- Evaluate: is the insurance revenue worth the administrative cost?
- Be willing to drop payers that aren't worth it

## Financial Impact Estimates

### If Accepting Insurance (Option B — Regence + Providence)

Assumptions: 20% of patients use insurance, average reimbursement $50/visit (1 unit), 80 insured visits/month

| Item | Monthly |
|------|---------|
| Insurance revenue (80 visits × $50) | $4,000 |
| Less: billing software/clearinghouse | -$150 |
| Less: billing time (10 hrs × $25/hr or outsourced 7%) | -$250–$280 |
| **Net insurance revenue** | **$3,570–$3,600** |
| Cash revenue (remaining 320 visits × $35) | $11,200 |
| **Total gross revenue** | **~$14,800** |

Compared to all-cash at 400 visits/month × $35 = $14,000. The insurance bump is modest (~$800/month net) but adds administrative complexity.

### The Real Value of Insurance

The financial case for insurance in community acupuncture is less about per-visit revenue and more about:
1. **Patient volume** — appearing in provider directories brings patients who would never find you otherwise
2. **Access** — some patients cannot come without insurance coverage, period
3. **OHP patients** — the most underserved patients have OHP, not cash
