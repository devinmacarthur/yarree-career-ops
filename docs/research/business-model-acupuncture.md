## Executive Summary

Using the POCA cooperative model — recliners in a shared room, sliding scale pricing, high volume — they can hit their $3,333/month income target within 6–9 months of opening, with startup costs under $6,000.

The economics of the POCA model are well-proven across 175+ clinics nationwide.

## Revenue Model

### Pricing: Sliding Scale
| Tier | Per Treatment | Who It's For |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Low end | $25 | Students, uninsured, low-income |
| Mid | $35 | Working-class, moderate income |
| High end | $50 | Those who can pay more (supports the scale) |
| **Blended average** | **$35** | **Use this for all projections** |

Patients self-select on the sliding scale. No verification, no questions. This is standard POCA practice.

### Volume Model
Community acupuncture treats **4–8 patients per hour** in a group room (recliners, shared space, quiet environment). Treatments run 30–60 minutes; patients arrive and leave on a rolling basis.

Conservative estimate: **5 patients per shift-hour** average.

### Revenue per Shift
| Shift Length | Patients | Revenue (@ $35 avg) |
|-------------|----------|---------------------|
| 3 hours | 15 | $525 |
| 4 hours | 20 | $700 |
| 5 hours | 25 | $875 |

## Break-Even Analysis

**Monthly overhead (midpoint):** ~$1,550
**Average revenue per treatment:** $35
**Treatments needed to cover overhead:** 45/month = ~11/week

At just 11 treatments per week, the practice covers its costs. Everything above that is income. This is why the POCA model works — the break-even is remarkably low.

**Treatments to hit $3,333/month net (after 25% tax reserve):**
- Need ~$4,883 gross ($3,333 net + $1,550 overhead = $4,883 before tax, then ÷ 0.75 = ~$6,510 gross)
- Wait — let's be precise:
- Target net: $3,333
- Gross needed: ($3,333 + $1,550 overhead) ÷ 0.75 = **$6,511/month**
- At $35/treatment: **186 treatments/month = ~47/week = ~9.4/shift (5 shifts)**

That's aggressive but achievable at capacity. More realistically, the combined income from acupuncture ($3,500–$4,500) + dog training ($800–$1,200) gets Yarrow to the $3,333 net target well before acupuncture alone could carry it.

**Realistic path to $3,333 net:**

| Source | Gross | After tax reserve (25%) |
|--------|-------|----------------------|
| Acupuncture (20 shifts/mo, 4 patients/hr avg, 4 hrs) | $2,800 | $2,100 |
| Dog Heart PDX | $1,000 | $750 |
| **Combined** | **$3,800** | **$2,850** |
| Less overhead | | -$1,550 |
| **Net** | | **$1,300** |

Hmm — that's Phase 2. The real $3,333 net from combined sources happens when acupuncture volume hits 5 patients/hr average across 5 shifts/week:

| Source | Gross | After expenses & tax |
|--------|-------|---------------------|
| Acupuncture (5 shifts × 4 hrs × 5 pts × $35) | $3,500 | |
| Dog Heart PDX | $1,000 | |
| **Total gross** | **$4,500** | |
| Less overhead | | -$1,550 |

To truly clear $3,333 net on acupuncture + dog training alone:
- Need ~$6,500 gross total

**Bottom line:** $3,333/month net is achievable in Phase 3 (month 10–18) when acupuncture is at moderate-high volume and dog training continues.

## Legal & Business Structure

### Recommended: Sole Proprietorship → LLC

**Start as sole prop** (simplest, cheapest):
- Portland business license: ~$100/year
- Oregon business registry: ~$100
- EIN from IRS: free
- Open business bank account

**Convert to LLC when revenue is consistent** (Year 2):
- Oregon LLC filing: $100
- Provides liability protection separate from malpractice insurance
- Consult a CPA on timing — the tax benefits of LLC (S-corp election) kick in when net income exceeds ~$40K/year

### Insurance Needed
| Type | Cost | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| Malpractice (professional liability) | $300–$600/year | Required. Get through OAAM or AAC. |
| General liability | $500–$800/year | Covers slip-and-fall, property damage |
| Business property | Optional | If you own expensive equipment |

### Tax Planning
- **Quarterly estimated taxes** from day 1 (IRS Form 1040-ES)
- Set aside 25–30% of net income for federal + state + self-employment tax
- Track all business expenses (mileage, supplies, rent, CE, insurance)
- Find a CPA familiar with self-employed healthcare practitioners
- Deductible expenses: rent, supplies, insurance, CE, professional memberships, mileage, phone (% business use)

## Insurance Revenue Potential (Future Upside)

Oregon mandates acupuncture coverage on all commercial health plans (ORS 743A.078). The POCA model is designed as cash-pay, but insurance can be added selectively as the practice matures.

**Recommended phased approach:**
1. **Launch (Fall 2026):** Cash-only with superbills for patient out-of-network reimbursement
2. **Growth (Spring 2027):** Evaluate demand; begin credentialing with Regence + Providence if warranted
3. **Established (Fall 2027+):** Add OHP/Health Share for Medicaid patients; consider hybrid private room for insurance visits

**Revenue impact estimate:** Adding 2 insurers with ~80 insured visits/month at $50/visit = ~$3,600/month net after billing costs. Combined with cash revenue, this could push total gross to $14,000+/month at full capacity.

**Key tension:** Insurance adds $150–$300/month in billing costs plus significant documentation burden. The POCA model's low overhead advantage is partially eroded. Start with superbills (zero cost) and let patient demand guide the decision.
