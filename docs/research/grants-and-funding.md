## 1. Health Equity Grants (Oregon & National)

### Oregon Health Authority — Health Equity Grants
- **Source:** Oregon Health Authority (OHA), Office of Equity and Inclusion
- **Amount:** $10,000–$100,000 (varies by cycle)
- **Eligibility:** Organizations providing culturally responsive health services to underserved communities in Oregon. Community-based health providers serving populations experiencing health disparities. Yarrow's multilingual, trauma-informed, queer/trans-focused practice is a strong fit.
- **Deadline:** Typically annual RFP cycle (spring/summer). Watch OHA website.
- **URL:** https://www.oregon.gov/oha/OEI/Pages/index.aspx
- **Notes:** OHA has historically funded acupuncture and integrative medicine under health equity umbrellas. Even if structured as a sole proprietor, Yarrow may qualify if serving documented underserved populations.

### Oregon Community Foundation — Community Grants
- **Source:** Oregon Community Foundation (OCF)
- **Amount:** $5,000–$50,000
- **Eligibility:** Nonprofit organizations or fiscally sponsored projects serving Oregon communities. Yarrow would need either 501(c)(3) status or a fiscal sponsor.
- **Deadline:** Rolling and cyclical — multiple grant rounds per year
- **URL:** https://oregoncf.org/grants-and-scholarships/
- **Notes:** OCF has funded health equity and wellness access projects. A fiscal sponsor like POCA or a local nonprofit could make this accessible.

### HRSA — Health Center Program / Community Health Center Grants
- **Source:** Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA), federal
- **Amount:** $100,000+ (larger scale, typically for established orgs)
- **Eligibility:** Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) and look-alikes. Not directly applicable to a solo startup, but relevant if Yarrow partners with or works within an existing FQHC that wants to add acupuncture services.
- **URL:** https://www.hrsa.gov/grants
- **Notes:** Better as a long-term strategy. Some Portland FQHCs (e.g., Outside In, Old Town Clinic) have integrated acupuncture; Yarrow could approach them about contracted work that comes with grant-funded compensation.

### Northwest Health Foundation
- **Source:** Northwest Health Foundation
- **Amount:** $5,000–$50,000
- **Eligibility:** Community-based organizations in Oregon and SW Washington advancing health equity. Preference for projects addressing social determinants of health. Fiscal sponsorship accepted.
- **Deadline:** Varies by program — typically 1–2 cycles per year
- **URL:** https://www.nwhf.org/grants
- **Notes:** Strong alignment with Yarrow's mission. They fund "upstream" health interventions and community-responsive care models. Worth reaching out to program staff before applying.

### Robert Wood Johnson Foundation — Culture of Health Prize / Grants
- **Source:** Robert Wood Johnson Foundation (RWJF)
- **Amount:** Varies ($25,000–$500,000 depending on program)
- **Eligibility:** Organizations building health equity. Typically requires nonprofit status. Competitive nationally.
- **URL:** https://www.rwjf.org/en/grants.html
- **Notes:** More realistic if Yarrow partners with a larger org or applies after establishing a track record. Good for Phase 2 expansion funding.

## 2. Small Business Grants — Oregon & Portland

### Prosper Portland — Small Business Programs
- **Source:** Prosper Portland (City of Portland economic development agency)
- **Amount:** Varies by program — grants from $2,500–$25,000; technical assistance is free
- **Eligibility:** Portland-based small businesses, with priority for BIPOC, women, and underserved entrepreneurs. Wellness and healthcare businesses qualify.
- **Programs to explore:**
  - **Inclusive Business Resource Network (IBRN):** Free business advising, classes, and connections to capital. Multiple partner orgs.
  - **Mercatus Platform grants:** Periodic small business grants ($2,500–$10,000)
  - **Neighborhood Prosperity Initiative (NPI):** District-specific grants for businesses in designated areas (Cully, Jade District, Lents, etc.)
- **URL:** https://www.prosperportland.us/
- **Notes:** Yarrow should register with Prosper Portland's IBRN immediately. The advising is free and connects to grant opportunities as they open. Cully and Lents are NPI districts and match Yarrow's target neighborhoods.

### Oregon RAIN (Regional Accelerator & Innovation Network) **(VERIFY)**
- **Source:** RAIN
- **Amount:** Varies — primarily mentorship, some seed funding
- **Eligibility:** Oregon-based startups and small businesses
- **URL:** https://oregonrain.org/
- **Notes:** More tech-focused but has supported health/wellness ventures. Worth exploring.

### Multnomah County — Small Business Stabilization Grants
- **Source:** Multnomah County
- **Amount:** $2,500–$10,000 (when active)
- **Eligibility:** Multnomah County small businesses, priority for historically underserved entrepreneurs
- **Deadline:** Periodic — tied to county funding cycles
- **URL:** https://www.multco.us/ (search "small business grants")
- **Notes:** These grants open sporadically, often tied to federal pass-through funding. Worth monitoring.

### Oregon Business Development Department — Small Business Grants
- **Source:** Business Oregon (state agency)
- **Amount:** Varies by program
- **Eligibility:** Oregon-based businesses, sometimes sector-specific
- **URL:** https://www.oregon.gov/biz/
- **Notes:** Business Oregon maintains a grant/incentive finder tool. Worth checking quarterly.

### IDA (Individual Development Account) — Oregon IDA Initiative
- **Source:** Neighborhood Partnerships / Oregon IDA Initiative
- **Amount:** 3:1 match on savings, up to $3,000 saved = $9,000 match ($12,000 total)
- **Eligibility:** Low-to-moderate income Oregonians starting or expanding a small business. Must complete financial education classes. Income limits apply (typically 200% of federal poverty level).
- **Deadline:** Rolling enrollment, but slots fill — apply early
- **URL:** https://oregonidainitiative.org/ or https://neighborhoodpartnerships.org/
- **Notes:** THIS IS A TOP RECOMMENDATION. If Yarrow qualifies by income, the 3:1 match could turn $3,000 in savings into $12,000 — covering the entire startup budget plus reserve. The required financial education classes are genuinely useful. Multiple Portland-area host organizations administer IDAs (e.g., MESO, CASA of Oregon, Hacienda CDC).

## 3. POCA Cooperative Resources

### POCA (People's Organization of Community Acupuncture)
- **Source:** POCA Cooperative
- **Programs:**
  - **CA101 Wiki & Forums:** Free startup guidance, business templates, operational playbooks for opening a community acupuncture clinic. This is the most valuable single resource for clinic startup.
  - **POCA Tech Scholarship pipeline:** If Yarrow graduated through a POCA-affiliated program, there may be alumni support networks.
  - **Clinic Circle Mentorship:** Established POCA clinic owners mentor new practitioners. Informal but high-value.
  - **POCA Cooperative Membership ($50–$200/year):** Access to bulk purchasing, shared resources, and community.
- **Direct financial assistance:** POCA does not typically offer direct grants or loans, but the cooperative network provides in-kind support (advice, templates, referrals) that reduces startup costs significantly.
- **URL:** https://www.pocacoop.com/
- **Notes:** Yarrow is already a POCA member. They should maximize the CA101 Wiki, attend POCA Fest if possible, and connect with Portland-area POCA clinic owners (Working Class Acupuncture is the founding clinic and is local).

### POCA Tech (Portland)
- **Source:** POCA Technical Institute
- **Amount:** N/A (educational institution)
- **URL:** https://pocatech.org/
- **Notes:** If Yarrow is completing their training at POCA Tech (now Oregon College of Community Acupuncture or similar), alumni networks and institutional connections are a resource for finding space, mentorship, and possibly introductions to funders.

## 6. Microloans & Alternative Financing

### Mercy Corps Northwest — Business Microloans
- **Source:** Mercy Corps Northwest (Portland-based)
- **Amount:** $500–$100,000 (microloans start at $500)
- **Eligibility:** Oregon and Washington small businesses. No minimum credit score for smallest loans. Specifically serves entrepreneurs who cannot access traditional bank loans.
- **Interest rate:** Typically 8–15% depending on risk profile
- **URL:** https://www.mercycorpsnw.org/
- **Notes:** TOP RECOMMENDATION for loan financing. They provide both capital and free business advising. Very startup-friendly. Yarrow's $3,000–$6,000 range is perfect for their microloan program.

### Craft3
- **Source:** Craft3 (Pacific Northwest CDFI)
- **Amount:** $5,000–$3,000,000 (small business loans start at $5,000)
- **Eligibility:** Oregon and Washington businesses. Mission-driven lending — prioritizes underserved communities, health, and sustainability.
- **Interest rate:** Below-market rates, varies
- **URL:** https://www.craft3.org/
- **Notes:** Good option if Yarrow needs slightly more capital (e.g., $5,000–$15,000 for buildout). They understand mission-driven healthcare businesses.

### Kiva — 0% Interest Crowdfunded Loans
- **Source:** Kiva (global microlending platform, active in Portland)
- **Amount:** Up to $15,000
- **Eligibility:** Any US-based entrepreneur. No credit score minimum. Loans are crowdfunded by individual lenders.
- **Interest rate:** 0% (yes, zero)
- **Repayment:** Begins after a grace period, typically 6 months
- **URL:** https://www.kiva.org/borrow
- **Notes:** TOP RECOMMENDATION. Zero-interest loans up to $15K. Yarrow would create a Kiva profile telling their story, and community members fund the loan. The community acupuncture narrative — sliding scale, queer/trans health, multilingual — is exactly the kind of story that does well on Kiva. Portland has a very active Kiva hub (Kiva Portland).

### Kiva Portland Hub
- **Source:** Kiva City Portland partnership
- **Amount:** Same as Kiva above
- **URL:** https://www.kivaportland.org/ **(VERIFY)**
- **Notes:** Local support for Kiva borrowers — help with applications, local networking events, and endorsement that helps loans fund faster.

### MESO (Micro Enterprise Services of Oregon)
- **Source:** MESO
- **Amount:** Microloans $1,000–$50,000; also provides free business classes and advising
- **Eligibility:** Oregon-based entrepreneurs, especially low-income, immigrant, and underserved business owners
- **URL:** https://www.mesopdx.org/
- **Notes:** MESO also administers IDA accounts (see Section 2). They offer classes in English, Spanish, and other languages. Yarrow could benefit from both their lending and technical assistance.

### Community Lending Works (formerly NEDCO)
- **Source:** Community Lending Works
- **Amount:** Microloans $500–$50,000
- **Eligibility:** Oregon small businesses
- **URL:** https://communitylendingworks.org/
- **Notes:** Another CDFI option with flexible terms for startups.

### SBA Microloans (via intermediary lenders)
- **Source:** U.S. Small Business Administration, disbursed through local intermediaries (Mercy Corps NW, MESO, etc.)
- **Amount:** Up to $50,000
- **Eligibility:** Small businesses and sole proprietors
- **Interest rate:** 8–13%
- **URL:** https://www.sba.gov/funding-programs/loans/microloans
- **Notes:** SBA microloans are disbursed through the local lenders listed above. Applying directly to Mercy Corps NW or MESO is the easiest path.

### Fiscal Sponsor Decision Point
Many of the best grants (Tiers 2–3) require 501(c)(3) status or a fiscal sponsor. Yarrow should decide early whether to:
- **(A)** Stay sole proprietor — focus on Tier 1 (IDA, Kiva, microloans, Prosper Portland)
- **(B)** Get a fiscal sponsor (5–10% fee) — unlocks foundation grants (Pride Foundation, Astraea, OCF, Collins, etc.)
- **(C)** Incorporate as nonprofit — maximum grant access but more administrative overhead

Recommendation: Start as **(A)**, pursue a fiscal sponsor **(B)** within 6 months if grant opportunities justify the 5–10% fee.
